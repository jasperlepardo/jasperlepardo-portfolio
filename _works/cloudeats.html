Introduction

CloudEats is a cloud kitchen company operating multiple delivery-only brands in a shared, high-volume kitchen environment. The Kitchen Display System (KDS) is the team’s central hub for managing orders—but it relied heavily on color to indicate status, making it difficult for color-blind staff to keep up during peak hours.

My challenge: redesign the KDS to ensure order clarity for all team members, especially under pressure and across varying types of color vision.

Approach

This was a focused, accessibility-first sprint. I followed a lean UX process:

  • Conducted on-site observations and staff interviews across multiple CloudEats kitchens
  • Simulated color blindness conditions to identify failure points
  • Audited the existing UI for over-reliance on color and lack of contrast
  • Prototyped a pattern-based status system inspired by Trello’s color-blind labels
  • Ran usability tests with color-blind and neurotypical team members

Design Enhancements

From Visual Guesswork to Fast, Pattern-Based Clarity

Redundant Visual Cues

Introduced pattern overlays for each status (e.g., stripes, dots, grids) in addition to color, making states unmistakable under any vision condition.

Contrast and Icon Clarity

Enhanced UI contrast and icon visibility to support quick scanning in dim kitchen environments.

nclusive by Default

Rolled out the new visual system across all KDS screens without requiring users to toggle accessibility modes—baking inclusion into the base experience.

Problem

Why the Original Design Didn’t Work for CloudEats' Color-Blind Staff

Status Confusion During Rush Hours

Users with red-green or blue-yellow color blindness struggled to distinguish key states like “In Progress” vs “Ready.”

Low Lighting + Speed = Errors

Poor visibility combined with high-pressure workflows led to frequent misreads and order delays.

Reflection

Designing for color blindness at CloudEats reminded me that accessibility and performance aren’t at odds. By borrowing from proven models like Trello and grounding decisions in real kitchen feedback, we created a system that feels smarter, faster, and more fair for every team member.

This project also reaffirmed my belief in inclusive design as a baseline—not an optional layer.
